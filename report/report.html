Its production database holds something no external vendor can obtain — every player's behaviour joined to their revenue. 1. Target Customer

The customer is the solo founder or 2–5-person studio shipping a free-to-play social or casual mobile game — the segment that lives on in-app purchases but has no data analyst and no data engineer. Concretely: 10k–500k MAU, one or two engineers doing everything, monetization set by guesswork.

The job they cannot do today

They sit on the exact data Peeps has — a behavioural database plus a RevenueCat/App Store revenue feed — but cannot answer the two questions that decide their revenue: "why do my payers pay, and who else will?" and "should I chase more payers, or more spend per payer?" Their status quo is a generic analytics dashboard (RevenueCat, GameAnalytics, Firebase) that reports what happened and never recommends what to do. A senior data hire to close that gap costs ~US$120k/yr — unjustifiable to set a $4.99 price.

Why this is the right wedge

  • Acute pain, no in-house fix — too small to hire a data team, too revenue-dependent to guess.
  • They already own the data — we supply the missing intelligence layer, not the data, so there is no acquisition cost or privacy transfer.
  • Output is dollars — the system ends in a ranked $-upside list, so willingness to pay is trivial to anchor (§4).

2.2 Why players pay — a wagering loop, not cosmetics

The dominant pcoin sink is a competitive wagering economy: players bet pcoins in head-to-head minigames (SPEED_CHALLENGE, GOMOKU, LOTTERY, FIGHTING) — tens of millions of pcoins in pots. Cosmetics are effectively dead (only 0.3% of players own any). The causal signature is stark:

86.3% of payers had wagered pcoins before their first purchase, and 56.4% buy on day 0. Players buy currency to keep betting — so the conversion moment is the pcoin balance running low mid-game, not a cosmetics storefront.

2.3 Who pays — the gambling mechanics, not the demographics

Cross-tabbing paying against each player's betting economy (net pcoins won − wagered, per-player, over 1M sessions) reveals that variance, not play, is the monetizer — a J-curve, not a line:

Big loser (net < −5k)1,1046.25%

Even (the casual majority)84,7692.20%

The casual "even" middle — 49% of the base — barely pays (2.20%). Money sits at both extremes: big losers refill (loss-chasing), big winners are deeply-engaged high-rollers. Stake size confirms it: payer rate rises 2.26% → 3.97% → 4.91% as average bet grows. And the two paying groups are psychologically opposite:

GroupAvg wageredNet pcoinsMotivation
Whales ($75+)5,470+298 (net winner)high-roller; bets big and wins
Regular payers3,694−308 (net loser)loses, then refills to keep playing

This is the core "who pays" result: the product must serve two psychologies — high-stakes lanes for whales, and fast cheap top-ups at the loss moment for refillers. Skill/action games (SPEED_CHALLENGE 3.13%, FIGHTING 3.11%) monetize better than pure chance (LOTTERY 2.71%). On the demand side, surveyed non-payers name their barrier directly: 844 "not yet" (activation-ready), 574 "too expensive", 433 "nothing worth" — splitting the non-payer problem into activation vs. price/value.

2.4 Stated vs revealed willingness to pay (per player)

Joining the in-app survey (stated) to real purchases (revealed) at the player level resolves a question the survey alone cannot:

SignalPayer rate (low)Payer rate (high)Lift
Wagered pcoins (>0)0.61%2.89%4.7×

Stated intent is predictive (3.9× ranking lift) but absolute conversion is tiny — most who say "yes" never pay — so pricing must be set off revealed behaviour, and observed behaviour (wagering, 4.7×) outranks self-report. This is the core methodological result: discount words, trust behaviour.

2.5 Competitor pricing & the customer's willingness to pay

Incumbents are descriptive, not prescriptive: RevenueCat / GameAnalytics / Firebase / Amplitude report metrics; none output "which lever, which players, how many dollars". Pricing-research tools (Conjoint.ly, Qualtrics) are survey platforms decoupled from the studio's revenue. For a single app this system surfaces ~$17k/yr of ranked, addressable upside (§3.4); priced at $99–$299/month it costs a small fraction of the revenue it recovers — a clear willingness-to-pay anchor.

3. Technical System

A SQL-first pipeline on the production database: ingest two first-party sources, process them into a per-player feature store and an analysis suite, and serve a diagnostic to a dashboard and an LLM agent. Heavy joins (1M+ sessions, 5.8M feedings) run in Postgres; Python only orchestrates and serializes.

INGEST
Production Postgres — player, survey_response, pcoin_transaction, prime_subscription_event, game_session, player_room … (173k players, restored from a 47.5 GB dump).
RevenueCat — revenue source-of-truth, joined at player.id = app_user_id. Reconciles with the DB.

3.1 Data sources & ingestion

Two first-party streams: the production Postgres (behaviour + surveys) and RevenueCat (revenue). In this case study both are batched (a restored dump + an API pull); in production each is a change-data-capture / event stream into an object store, so the feature store rebuilds incrementally and refunds/late surveys reprocess idempotently.

3.2 Storage & processing — why SQL fits

The binding constraint is joining many large behavioural tables per player (1.0M game sessions, 5.8M pet feedings, 213k room memberships), not exotic compute. A set-based SQL feature store collapses each source per player before joining, so the whole pipeline runs in seconds and is trivially portable to a distributed warehouse (BigQuery / Spark SQL) by repartitioning on player_id — no logic change. The propensity model is a transparent additive score (weights on wagering, streak, activity, virality, stated intent) so a non-technical studio can audit why a player scored high; it is validated on a 20% holdout it was not tuned on.

3.4 Delivery — the diagnostic (headline output)

The system's one-line answer to "convert more vs spend more": it sizes every lever on real pools and ranks them.

Directional upside on real pools, to be confirmed by live A/B. For Peeps the answer is "both, but PRIME first" — the subscription is barely launched (~150 real subscribers among 3.3k payers).

3.5 Scalability & cost (10× / 100×)

Per studio the marginal cost is cents (nightly SQL + a few LLM calls). At 10–100 studios the feature store becomes a multi-tenant warehouse partitioned by studio; the per-player set-based logic is unchanged. Value scales with each studio's own data, so there is no shared-data cold-start across tenants.

4. Go-to-Market Difficulties (bonus)

ObstacleHonest assessment
Trust & adoptionThe system never asserts a change — it proposes an A/B and reports the revealed result, so the studio's own data decides.
Wagering & regulationMonetization rides a pcoin wagering loop, and the data shows loss-chasing is its strongest signal (big losers pay 2.8× the casual baseline). Promoting that invites responsible-design / gambling-adjacent scrutiny (minors, odds disclosure, store policy). The system must flag vulnerable loss-chasers and exclude minors, not amplify them — an ethics constraint baked into the targeting, not an afterthought.
Data privacy (PDPA/GDPR)We process inside the studio's own tenancy on pseudonymous IDs; a PII gate keeps player_id/email out of every output. The studio stays the data controller.
Cold-startNeeds the studio's own behaviour+revenue; a new game has little. Onboard with a lightweight survey SDK; revealed data comes from their existing RevenueCat.
Competition & moatGeneric analytics could add a recommendation layer; defensibility is the workflow (owning the join + the diagnostic for studios with no data team), not the (deliberately simple) model.
Unit economics~$17k/yr ranked upside for one mid-size app; at $199/mo the product costs ~14% of recovered revenue with near-zero marginal cost — high gross margin once onboarding is automated.

5. Conclusion

Raw data had no value here; 173k rows read naïvely would have chased cosmetics and trusted a survey that overstates intent. Value appeared only after the data was refined — two first-party sources joined per player, the wagering driver isolated, stated intent discounted to a revealed 1.92% base rate, a holdout-validated score built, and the result handed to an agent that proposes a test rather than a dogma. That refined output — a ranked, dollar-sized list of which lever to pull and which players to action — is something a studio with no data team would genuinely pay for. The system is modest by design; the judgment it encodes is the product.

Reproducibility

Restore the dump into local Postgres, then python3 src/run_pipeline.py (builds the feature store + runs all SQL → outputs/insights.json), python3 agent/recommend.py, and open dashboard/index.html. Analytics run in SQL; the agent uses the Anthropic SDK with a deterministic offline fallback. A PII gate enforces aggregates-only; the raw production dump never leaves the gitignored data/_private/.
